# 09 - Session Recap Magic Guide

## Purpose

Teach the model how to turn verified Para League facts into player-facing league prose without inventing facts.

This guide is not permission to fabricate emotion, rivalry, table talk, private strategy, or standings movement. It is permission to use sequence, contrast, pressure, and consequence when those are supported by the supplied record.

## 1. Do Not Open With Metadata

Avoid opening with metadata-first lines:

- "Session S0-001 launched..."
- "The session concluded..."
- "On June 15..."
- "In a 70-hand session..."

Prefer opening with:

- the turning point
- the pressure swing
- the winner's earned shape
- the non-winner's resistance
- what the session gave the standings/archive

The first sentence should make a reader feel why the recap exists before it lists the session file.

## 2. Every Recap Needs Story Shape

Every public session recap should carry this shape:

- Hook: what gave the session its identity?
- Result: who earned the night?
- Turning point: what hand or sequence changed the texture?
- Worthy resistance: what did the non-winner still show?
- Consequence: what does the league record now know?

Do not treat these as visible labels. They are the hidden structure underneath the prose.

## 3. Drama Comes From Verified Sequence And Consequence

Allowed language when supported by the facts:

- "played uphill"
- "set the pace"
- "gave the session its shape"
- "left a marker"
- "kept the runner-up line alive"
- "the first standings line has a name"

Avoid only what breaks the record: fake emotions, fake rivalries, fake destiny, invented hand action, invented standings movement, and database/report language in public prose.

Use "because" energy without inventing a because. Example: a player can "play uphill" after losing the largest available pot; do not say they felt pressure unless the data says so.

## 4. Two-Player Sessions

For two-player sessions, do not say "field" unless there was an actual field.

Prefer:

- table
- session
- opener
- first board
- first standings line

A two-player opener can still matter. It just needs honest language.

## 5. Keep Internal Language Out

Public recap prose should not expose newsroom plumbing. Keep these ideas in admin notes, not the article body:

- "processed"
- "generated"
- "source facts"
- "visibility"
- "draft"

Admin notes may discuss confidence and missing context. The public body should sound like the table, not the review queue.

## 6. S0-001 Rewritten Examples

### Serious Article Version

Hand #6 gave S0-001 its first real weight. Para-Poker pulled in 15,290 chips, the largest pot in the available record, and turned the opener into more than a result line. From there, the first standings line had a name: Para-Poker finished first for 10 points.

panicmixie still kept the session from reading empty. The runner-up finish brought 7 points, and the late pots in the record kept their side of the table alive even after the largest swing had gone the other way.

S0-001 does not need to pretend to be a full season. It gave Para League a starting mark: Para-Poker at the top, panicmixie close enough to matter, and one early hand big enough to carry the recap.

### Punchier Recap-Card Version

The opener turned on Hand #6. Para-Poker took 15,290 chips, finished first, and put the first 10-point mark on the board. panicmixie answered enough to keep second place alive, but S0-001 left with Para-Poker's name on the first standings line.

### Player-Facing Version

Para-Poker's first mark came loud: first place, 10 points, and the biggest pot from S0-001. Hand #6 did the heavy lifting, moving 15,290 chips their way and giving the win a clear center.

panicmixie did not disappear from the record. Seven points and a runner-up finish kept their opener meaningful, with enough late-session presence to make the next meeting worth watching.

That is the right size of the story: one opener, two players, one defining pot, and a first standings line the league can build from.
